# Incident Management Process — Review Backlog

> **Review conducted by**: tcl-cio (CIO Review)
> **Date**: 2026-03-18
> **Scope**: Full version (`swimlane-flow.md`) and Lightweight version (`swimlane-flow.light.md`), cross-referenced with `classification.md`, `incident-knowledge-base.md`, and `scenarios.md`
> **Context**: IT incident management process for TCLife, a life insurance company

---

## CIO Review Summary

The documentation is strong for a first iteration. The swimlane flow is well-structured, roles are clearly defined, and the split between infrastructure and application incidents is appropriate for a life insurance operation. The classification guide and scenarios are particularly well done — practical, not theoretical.

However, several gaps exist that must be addressed before this process is operational. The gaps fall into five categories: **regulatory and compliance**, **business continuity**, **security incident handling**, **operational readiness**, and **metrics and governance**. These are not nice-to-haves — for a life insurance company regulated by insurance authorities, several of these are audit-critical.

## Backlog Items

### P1 — Must Address Before Operational Use

| ID | Item | Description | Rationale |
|----|------|-------------|-----------|
| IM-001 | **Regulatory notification procedure** | The classification doc mentions regulatory triggers (personal data exposure, financial transactions incorrectly processed, core systems down >4 hours, events affecting policyholder rights). But the swimlane flow has NO step for regulatory notification. There is no decision point asking "does this trigger a regulatory filing?" and no lane or responsibility assignment for it. Create a regulatory notification decision gate within Stage 3 (Respond) and Stage 5 (Close), with clear ownership (Compliance Officer or designated role), specific timelines per regulation, and template notifications. | Life insurance companies are typically required to notify regulators within defined windows (often 24-72 hours) for material incidents. Missing a filing deadline is itself a compliance violation. |
| IM-002 | **Security incident pathway** | The classification doc lists "Security / IAM" as an escalation path and "data breach / security event" as a P1 trigger. But the swimlane flow has no dedicated security incident handling. A data breach at a life insurance company involves policyholder PII, health data, and financial records — it requires a fundamentally different response: forensic preservation, legal hold, privacy officer notification, law enforcement coordination, and customer notification under data protection laws. Create a security incident branch in the swimlane that forks from the triage decision, with its own response steps, roles (CISO, Privacy Officer, Legal), and regulatory notification timeline. | Security incidents at an insurer carrying policyholder health and financial data have legal obligations that differ from standard IT incidents. Treating a data breach like a normal infra incident will result in missed legal obligations. |
| IM-003 | **Disaster recovery and BCP integration** | The process covers incident response but has no linkage to business continuity plans (BCP) or disaster recovery (DR). If a P1 incident escalates beyond the response team's ability to resolve — e.g., a data center outage, ransomware attack, or prolonged vendor failure — there is no trigger to activate DR/BCP. Add a decision gate in Stage 3: "Has containment SLA been breached?" If yes, trigger DR/BCP activation with defined criteria and handoff procedure. Reference the DR plan and BCP. | Insurance regulators expect documented integration between incident management and BCP. An incident that exceeds containment SLA without DR activation leaves the company operating without a plan. |
| IM-004 | **Customer and policyholder communication plan** | The communication checkpoints cover internal parties (reporter, management, stakeholders) but say nothing about communicating with policyholders, agents, brokers, or distribution partners during a major incident. For a life insurer, a P1 incident affecting the customer portal, claims processing, or premium collection directly impacts policyholders. Define customer communication triggers, channels (portal banner, SMS, email, agent notification), templates, approval chain, and timing. This should be a responsibility of the IC with Marketing/Comms support. | Policyholders are not just "users" — they are contractual counterparties. Regulatory guidance in most jurisdictions requires timely communication when policyholder services are materially impacted. |
| IM-005 | **On-call and after-hours coverage model** | The process mentions "after hours — L1 + on-call dev assess; BA joins next business day" in a single note under Stage 2b. There is no defined on-call rotation, escalation path for after-hours, response time expectations outside business hours, or tooling (PagerDuty, OpsGenie, etc.). For P1 incidents, the SLA is measured in clock time (24/7). Without a defined on-call model, who gets paged at 2 AM? Document the on-call rotation model, after-hours escalation path, response time expectations for each priority outside business hours, and tools used for alerting. | A life insurance company processes overnight batches, handles time-sensitive claims, and may have regulatory reporting deadlines that don't respect business hours. A P1 at midnight with no defined on-call model means no response. |
| IM-006 | **Referenced documents do not exist** | The swimlane flow references seven documents (Incident Triage Guide, Incident Response Playbook, Change Management Policy, Escalation Policy, Post-Incident Review Template, Priority & Severity Matrix). None of these appear to exist yet. The Knowledge Base exists but is empty (only examples). The Classification doc partially covers some of these topics but is not the same as having the dedicated documents. Create the referenced documents or explicitly map which sections of existing documents serve as substitutes. The process cannot be followed if the references lead nowhere. | A process that references non-existent documents is not executable. During a P1 incident, responders will look for the "Incident Response Playbook" and find nothing. |

### P2 — Should Address Before Go-Live

| ID | Item | Description | Rationale |
|----|------|-------------|-----------|
| IM-007 | **Data remediation and rollback procedures** | Step 3.4d mentions "remediate bad data if applicable" as a sub-bullet. For a life insurance company, data remediation is a major activity — recalculating premiums, correcting policy values, reversing transactions, regenerating statements. This deserves its own procedure with: who approves data remediation, how to validate corrected data, how to handle data that has already been sent downstream (regulators, reinsurers, banks), and audit trail requirements. | Scenario 5 in the scenarios doc illustrates this perfectly: 347 policies with zero surrender value heading to a regulatory report. "Remediate bad data" is not a bullet point — it's a regulated activity that requires actuarial sign-off and audit trails. |
| IM-008 | **Reinsurer and third-party data recipient notification** | No mention of notifying reinsurers, banks, or other third parties who may have received incorrect data during an incident. Life insurers share data with reinsurers (treaty and facultative), payment processors, regulators, and actuarial consultants. If an incident corrupts data that has been transmitted, those parties need to be notified. Add a post-incident check: "Was incorrect data transmitted to external parties?" with notification procedures for each party type. | Sending corrected data to a reinsurer without explaining the original error can create treaty disputes. Regulators who receive corrected filings without explanation may initiate investigations. |
| IM-009 | **Incident management tooling specification** | The process mentions "ITSM" and "auto-create ticket" but does not specify which tool. No mention of: incident communication tool (Slack, Teams), war room platform, status page, monitoring dashboards, alerting tool, or knowledge base platform. The process is tool-agnostic, which is fine for a design document, but operational readiness requires specifying the tooling stack. Create a tooling annex that maps each process step to the tool used. | During a P1 incident, responders need to know where to go. "Establish War Room" means nothing if nobody knows whether that's a Zoom bridge, a Teams channel, or a physical room. |
| IM-010 | **P3/P4 process in swimlane flow** | The swimlane flow is designed around P1/P2 incidents. P3/P4 handling is covered in classification.md but not in the swimlane. The lightweight version also omits P3/P4. Since P3/P4 represent the majority of incidents, the swimlane flow should either include a simplified P3/P4 path or explicitly reference the P3/P4 handling section in classification.md from the swimlane decision points. | Most incidents are P3/P4. If the only documented process flow is for P1/P2, L1 staff may either over-escalate P3/P4 incidents or not know what to do with them. |
| IM-011 | **Incident reopening criteria** | No mention of what happens if an incident recurs after closure. If the same issue reappears within 24/48/72 hours, is it a new incident or a reopened one? This matters for SLA measurement, RCA scope, and trend analysis. Define reopening criteria: same root cause within X hours = reopen, otherwise = new incident linked to original. | Recurring incidents that are logged as separate tickets hide systemic problems. A policy calculation error that recurs three times should be tracked as one problem, not three independent incidents. |
| IM-012 | **Handoff procedures between shifts and teams** | No documentation of how incident ownership transfers between shifts (day to night, weekday to weekend) or between teams (L1 to IC, IC to tech team). During a P1 that spans multiple shifts, critical context can be lost at handoff. Define handoff checklist: current status, actions taken, pending actions, open questions, next update time, and who the new owner is. | A P1 incident that starts at 4 PM and runs past midnight will involve at least one shift handoff. Without a structured handoff, the night team starts from scratch. |
| IM-013 | **Training and certification requirements** | No mention of how staff are trained on this process, how competency is verified, or how often the process is exercised. For a life insurance company, regulators may ask for evidence that incident management staff are trained. Define: initial training program, annual refresher, tabletop exercise schedule (minimum quarterly for P1 scenarios), and records of participation. | An untested process is not a process. Tabletop exercises are the only way to find gaps before a real P1 hits. Insurance regulators increasingly expect evidence of incident response exercises. |
| IM-014 | **Lightweight version alignment verification** | The light version states it omits time targets, KB checks, major incident activation, L3/vendor escalation, QA validation, emergency change, communication checkpoints, collaboration stage, reporter confirmation, and document references. While this is expected for a presentation, the "Key Differences" table should be reviewed to ensure nothing was accidentally dropped that changes the process logic (vs. simply simplifying it). Specifically verify that the approval gate logic in the light version accurately reflects the full version. | The light version is likely to be shown to executives and external parties. If it inadvertently misrepresents the process (e.g., suggesting management approves ALL deployments when it's only P1/P2 app incidents), it creates confusion. |

### P3 — Improve Over Time

| ID | Item | Description | Rationale |
|----|------|-------------|-----------|
| IM-015 | **Metrics and KPI framework** | No definition of how incident management performance is measured. Define KPIs: MTTD (mean time to detect), MTTA (mean time to acknowledge), MTTR (mean time to resolve), SLA adherence rate, incident volume by priority/type/category, recurrence rate, RCA completion rate, action item closure rate. Define reporting cadence (weekly operational, monthly management, quarterly board). | What gets measured gets managed. Without KPIs, there is no way to demonstrate improvement, justify investment, or satisfy regulatory inquiries about operational resilience. |
| IM-016 | **Continuous improvement and process review cycle** | No defined cadence for reviewing and updating the incident management process itself. The knowledge base grows from RCAs, but the process document, classification guide, and scenarios should also be reviewed periodically. Define: quarterly review of process effectiveness based on KPIs, annual full process review, trigger-based review after any P1 that reveals a process gap. | A process that is never reviewed becomes stale. The scenarios doc is excellent but will need new scenarios as the application evolves. |
| IM-017 | **Integration with problem management** | The process mentions "Problem" in the ITIL mapping and glossary, but there is no formal linkage between incident management and problem management. After repeated incidents, who initiates a problem investigation? How are known errors formally documented and linked back to the KB? Define the trigger for creating a problem record (e.g., 3+ incidents with same root cause, or any P1 RCA that reveals a systemic issue). | Incident management without problem management is firefighting. The knowledge base is a step in the right direction, but formal problem management ensures root causes are eliminated, not just documented. |
| IM-018 | **Vendor management integration** | Scenario 4 covers vendor failures well, but the process has no formal linkage to vendor management. When a vendor-caused incident is resolved, who updates the vendor performance record? Who reviews vendor SLAs? Who decides if the vendor relationship needs escalation? Add a post-incident step for vendor-caused incidents: update vendor performance log, review against contracted SLA, escalate to vendor management if thresholds are breached. | Life insurers depend on multiple vendors (payment gateways, reinsurance platforms, actuarial tools, cloud providers). Without tracking vendor incident performance, the company cannot make informed decisions about vendor risk. |
| IM-019 | **Audit trail and evidence preservation** | The process does not specify what evidence must be preserved during and after an incident. For a regulated insurer, audit trails matter for: regulatory inquiries, internal audit, legal proceedings, and insurance fraud investigations. Define what must be preserved: ITSM ticket history, communication logs, system logs for the affected period, change records, data snapshots before and after remediation, and RCA documentation. Define retention period (aligned with regulatory requirements). | If a regulator asks "show me what happened during incident INC-0042," the company needs to produce a complete audit trail. Defining preservation requirements upfront prevents evidence loss. |
| IM-020 | **Capacity for concurrent major incidents** | The process assumes one major incident at a time. What happens if two P1 incidents occur simultaneously? Who is the IC for the second incident? How are resources allocated? Define: secondary IC designation, resource allocation protocol for concurrent incidents, and escalation path when the team is already at capacity. | While rare, concurrent major incidents do happen — especially during widespread infrastructure events or coordinated attacks. A process that breaks under load is not resilient. |
| IM-021 | **Knowledge base population plan** | The KB currently has only example entries. It is effectively empty. While the "How to Add an Entry" section is clear, there is no plan for initial population. Create a backfill plan: review past incidents (if any), conduct workshops with experienced staff to document known scenarios, and set a target for minimum KB entries before go-live (e.g., top 10 infra and top 10 app scenarios). | An empty knowledge base on day one means every incident is a "no KB match" incident, which triggers the longer collaboration path. Pre-populating with known scenarios reduces triage time from day one. |
